Basic B&W Film Developing Guide

This guide is an introduction to film developers. A lot of experimentation is possible, and the different results can be very noticeable. When starting out, it’s best to stick with common 400-speed films and common developers, because the more widespread something is, the more information is available. If you don't know where to start, then get Ilford HP5+, and develop with either Kodak D-76, Kodak HC-110 or Sprint Standard.

Basic Developing Questions

What does 1+1, 1+31, etc mean?

  • That value represents dilution. It does not represent ratio.
  • Example: "Sprint 1:9" means you have 50mL concentrate and 450mL water.
  • A high dilution let you save money by using less concentrate; the tradeoff is longer developing times and less contrast.

What is the correct way to agitate?

When I am developing film and I'm using some chemical, I go by Ilford's method, which is 2 inversions every 5 seconds. (see guide at ilford.com).

  • If the instructions say “agitate for 10 seconds,” then do 4 inversions.
  • If the instructions say “agitate for 30 seconds,” then do 12 inversions.
  • After doing inversions, tap the tank against the table to dislodge any air bubbles.
  • Kodak has slightly different rules for agitation. I've tried both and I can't tell the difference.

When do I start and stop the timer?

  • Ilford says you should start the timer as soon as all the chemicals go into the tank. Personally, I put on the drain cap and then I start the timer. The difference is a few seconds; it doesn't really matter.
  • Ilford says you should start pouring chemicals out of the tank 15 seconds before the timer ends. Personally, I start pouring things out after the timer stops, because that was how I was taught. The difference is a few seconds; it doesn't really matter.
  • I never develop anything under 5 minutes. If you consult a table and see something under 5 minutes, then you need to lower your developer's temperature.

Basic Developing Steps

These instructions are a combination of Ilford's official method and what I learned from Bob Laubach. Everyone has their own preferred way of doing things; under normal circumstances, it's pretty hard to spot the difference. If you feel anxious about getting things right, then stick with shooting only Ilford HP5+ for at least a full year. The most important thing is being consistent so that it's easier diagnose what went wrong if you don't get the results you wanted.

Order Step Notes
1 Prep
  • Set aside 3 cylinders of chemicals, in order:
  • 1: developer, 2: stop bath, 3: fixer
  • Ideally, you want to get your developer at 20°C or close to it.
2 Prewash
  • 60 seconds of water inversions
  • If you are shooting 120 film, you cannot skip the prewash
  • Pour out the water into the drain.
3 Develop
  • Pour chemicals into the tank. Start the timer
  • Agitate continuously for the first 30 seconds (about 12 inversions)
  • Let it rest.
  • At the beginning of each minute, agitate for 10 seconds (4 inversions)
  • When the timer stops, pour out the chemicals into the drain
4 Stop Bath
  • Pour Stop bath into the tank.
  • Start the timer: 30 seconds
  • Agitate continuously for 30 seconds.
  • Pour everything down the drain
5 Fixer
  • Pour fixer into the tank.
  • Start the timer: 5 minutes
  • Agitate continuously for the first 3 minutes, (about 30 inversions every minute)
  • Agitate for 10s every minute afterwards
  • When you are done, do not pour into the drain.
  • Pour it into the recycling drain.
6 Wash
  • Open the entire lid.
  • Start the timer for 5 minutes
  • Under a running sink, fill it up with water and let it sit for a few seconds
  • Pour everything out and repeat.
7 Final Rinse
  • You can skip this step but I never do
  • Start a timer for 60 seconds
  • Fill the tank with the wetting agent (e.g. Photo Flo)
  • Spin the reel a few rotations, and let it sit
8 Cleanup
  • Please be considerate of the people who use the lab after you.
  • Wash everything that you touched.
  • Wipe stuff dry.
  • Put everything away.

ISO 400 Films Summary

"Push/Pull" is the ability to use the same film but picking some arbitrary ISO to suit the lighting conditions. "Push" means you pick some higher ISO like 800; "pull" means you pick some lower ISO like 200. Very generally speaking, you want to push film when the scene is very flat like on a cloudy day or at a restaurant for dinner. You want to pull film when the scene is very contrasty, like staring into a sunrise.

Arista EDU Ultra 400

  • This film is not real. It's just relabeled Fomapan 400, for a few dollars cheaper.

Foma Fomapan 400

  • 4×5 is available.
  • This film is gritty and high contrast and probably not a true 400 speed film.
  • It has narrow dynamic range and it's very easy to mess up.
  • It has limited push/pull ability
  • It is objectively worse than most of the films on this page in every single way.
  • But it is the cheapest film, by a very wide margin.
  • It doesn't look that great if you use D-76, HC-110, or Sprint
  • Get this film if you already really know what you're doing

Ilford Delta 400

  • 4×5 is available.
  • This film has pretty good dynamic range, but less than Ilford HP5+
  • It has decent push/pull ability
  • It has really small grain compared to other ISO 400 films.
  • It's comparable to Kodak T-Max, but a little flatter and cheaper
  • It's cheaper than Kodak T-Max
  • Get this film if you very clean sharp images but don't want to pay for T-Max

Ilford HP5+ 400

  • 4×5 is available.
  • This film has amazing dynamic range; you can't mess it up
  • It has amazing push/pull ability
  • It's comparable to Kodak Tri-X but with less contrast, and cheaper
  • If you are a beginner, it is the best film
  • Get this film if you want something that can adapt to any condition.

Kentmere Pan 400

  • Does not have 4×5.
  • This film has decent dynamic range, and decent push/pull ability
  • Under ideal conditions, it's hard to distinguish it from Ilford HP5+
  • It's cheaper than HP5+
  • The film base is flimsy and difficult to scan or print
  • Get this film if you are okay with the drawbacks and if you can't afford HP5+.

Kodak T-Max

  • 4×5 is available.
  • If you are looking for the film that is technically the best, this is it
  • It has the smallest grain, the best sharpness, the best contrast
  • It's comparable to Ilford delta, but a little more contrast and costs more.
  • Kodak Tri-X has better dynamic range and better push/pull ability
  • It is more expensive than Tri-X
  • Get this film if technical quality matters, like studio shoots or wedding photos.

Kodak Tri-X

  • 4×5 is available for ISO 320. No 4×5 for ISO 400.
  • This is the best film for dynamic range, and has the best push/pull ability
  • Most of the street photographers in the past used this film
  • Do not compare current Tri-X photos to old Tri-X photos; the formulas have changed.
  • It's comparable to Ilford HP5+ but has more contrast and costs more
  • Get this film if you want to pretend to be a war photojournalist.
